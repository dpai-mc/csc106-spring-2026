Week 6 Additional Reading

CSC 106 – Introduction to Linux II  ·  Week 6  ·  Networking Fundamentals

The Week 6 module introduces networking concepts and hands-on diagnostic commands. These curated resources provide background reading and reference material to support that work. They are not required reading for every student — use them based on what you find most useful.

The Linux Essentials Chapter 14 reading and the Network Basics Check lab are your primary sources for this week. Think of the resources below as the reference shelf you reach for when you want a clearer explanation of a term, a different angle on a concept, or a framework for troubleshooting.

How to use these resources
  • Before the lab: The DigitalOcean terminology article is the best starting point if networking vocabulary feels unfamiliar. Read it before running the lab commands.
  • During the lab: Keep the GeeksForGeeks and Cloudflare pages open as references when you encounter terms or concepts you want to look up.
  • For extra credit: The DNSStuff troubleshooting article provides a structured framework that maps directly to the Network Troubleshooting Walkthrough extra credit assignment.
  • If a site is unreachable: See the Site Availability Note at the bottom of this page.
Resource 1

An Introduction to Networking Terminology, Interfaces, and Protocols

digitalocean.com — Networking Terminology, Interfaces, and Protocols

This DigitalOcean tutorial is the recommended starting point for students who are new to networking or whose networking vocabulary is shaky. It explains the concepts behind the commands you are running this week in plain, beginner-friendly language with practical examples.

If you have ever looked at ip addr output and been unsure what the numbers mean, or wondered why there are both IPv4 and IPv6 addresses, this article will fill in those gaps. It covers the vocabulary you need to understand what the Week 6 lab commands are actually showing you.

Topics covered
  • Network interfaces and how they are named on Linux
  • IP addresses, subnet masks, and CIDR notation
  • Routing and default gateways
  • DNS — how hostnames get translated to IP addresses
  • TCP and UDP ports and why services use numbered ports
  • Network protocols and the layered model
Connects to the lab: Phases 1–4 of the Network Basics Check (ip addr, ip route, ping, dig) map directly to the concepts explained here. Reading this article before completing the lab will make the output of those commands significantly easier to interpret.
Resource 2

Network Troubleshooting Steps

dnsstuff.com — Network Troubleshooting Steps

This article presents a structured, step-by-step methodology for diagnosing common network connectivity problems. Rather than guessing at solutions, experienced administrators follow a logical sequence: start at the lowest layer, confirm what is working, and progressively narrow down where the problem is. This article teaches that mindset.

The troubleshooting steps it describes map closely to the commands you used in the Network Basics Check lab — checking interface configuration, verifying routing, testing connectivity with ping, and confirming DNS resolution. If you are attempting the extra credit Network Troubleshooting Walkthrough, this article provides an excellent framework for structuring your response.

Topics covered
  • Systematic approach to network diagnostics (work layer by layer)
  • Checking physical and logical interface status
  • Verifying IP address and default gateway configuration
  • Using ping and traceroute to isolate the failing segment
  • Diagnosing DNS failures separately from connectivity failures
  • Common causes of "no internet" on a Linux server
Connects to the lab: The five commands in the Network Basics Check follow the same logical order as the troubleshooting steps described here — interface → route → connectivity → DNS → services. If any of your lab commands failed, this article will help you understand where in that chain the problem is and what to check next.
Resource 3

Basics of Computer Networking

geeksforgeeks.org — Basics of Computer Networking

GeeksForGeeks is a widely used technical reference site. This article gives a broad overview of computer networking fundamentals — how devices communicate, what role IP addresses play, how data travels from one machine to another, and how the layers of the networking model work together.

Use this as a reference when you encounter a networking concept or term you want to look up quickly. The site also has individual articles on specific topics (DNS, TCP, routing, ports) that you can navigate to from search or from links within the article.

Topics covered
  • What a computer network is and why it matters
  • Network types (LAN, WAN) and basic topologies
  • IP addressing and subnets
  • How packets travel from source to destination
  • The role of routers and switches
  • Introduction to protocols (TCP, UDP, IP, DNS, HTTP)
Connects to the lab: This article provides the conceptual foundation that explains why your VM needs an IP address, a default gateway, and working DNS — not just how to check them. It is also good background reading for Week 7, when you will configure a static IP, install a web server, and open firewall ports.
Resource 4

Cloudflare Learning Center

cloudflare.com/learning

The Cloudflare Learning Center is a large collection of short, clearly-written articles on networking, DNS, web security, and internet infrastructure. Cloudflare is one of the world's largest network providers, and their learning content is known for being technically accurate while remaining accessible to beginners.

There is no single assigned article here — instead, use this site as a reference when you want a plain-language explanation of a concept you encountered in the readings or the lab. The DNS and networking sections are the most directly relevant to Week 6. The security section will be useful again in Week 7 when you configure a firewall and harden SSH access.

Suggested starting points for Week 6
  • What is DNS? — Explains how name resolution works end to end; directly relevant to Phase 4 (dig)
  • What is an IP address? — Covers IPv4, IPv6, public vs. private addresses, and CIDR notation
  • What is a network port? — Explains why services use numbered ports; directly relevant to Phase 5 (ss -tlnp)
  • What is TCP/IP? — Overview of the protocol stack your Linux system uses for all network communication
Connects to the course: Cloudflare Learning is a resource you will continue to find useful beyond Week 6. Topics like firewalls, TLS/HTTPS, SSH, and DDoS protection are covered here and become relevant in Weeks 7 and 8.
Note

What to Do If a Site Is Unreachable

External websites occasionally go down or become temporarily unreachable — even major ones. For example, while assembling this resource list, ubuntu.com was unreachable due to what appeared to be an ongoing DNS attack on their servers. This is a real-world networking event, not an error on your end, and it is a good reminder that DNS problems affect everyone — even the people who write the documentation about DNS.

Before assuming a site is permanently gone or that you copied the URL incorrectly, use an online availability checker to see whether the problem is on your end or theirs. DownDetector is a widely used tool for this purpose. It shows real-time outage reports from users around the world for hundreds of services and websites.

downdetector.com — Website and Service Status Checker

To check a specific site, go to DownDetector and search for the service name. For Ubuntu specifically, the direct status page is downdetector.com/status/ubuntu/.

If a resource listed on this page is temporarily unavailable, try again later or search for the article title directly — many popular technical articles are mirrored, cached, or have been discussed on other platforms. If you believe a link on this page is permanently broken, let your instructor know through Canvas.
