Initial Post Examples

✓  Strong initial post — full credit
Before doing Lab 3, I knew runlevels existed but thought of them as just numbers. Working through systemd this week made the relationship click. Runlevels 0 through 6 in SysV init each mapped to a specific system state, and systemd targets are the modern equivalent. Runlevel 3 — multi-user with no GUI — corresponds to multi-user.target, which is exactly what my Ubuntu Server boots into by default. Running systemctl get-default confirmed this. Runlevel 1 — single-user mode — maps to rescue.target.

For a real rescue scenario: imagine you edited /etc/fstab and made a typo in a mount point entry. On the next reboot, the system cannot mount the filesystem and hangs before reaching the normal login prompt. You need rescue.target to get a root shell, fix the typo, and reboot normally. I actually simulated part of this in the lab — I ran sudo systemctl set-default rescue.target and rebooted. The system dropped into a minimal shell with no network services. It was quieter than I expected. Getting out required running systemctl set-default multi-user.target from inside the rescue shell before rebooting again. Now I understand why sysadmins treat rescue mode as a lifeline rather than something exotic.
~178 words ✓
Why this earns full credit
Both parts are addressed. The runlevel mapping is named specifically (runlevel 3 → multi-user.target, runlevel 1 → rescue.target). The rescue scenario is concrete: a real file, a real failure mode, a real fix. Actual commands from the lab are referenced. The final sentence reflects on what the experience meant, which is exactly the kind of depth the quality and depth rubric rewards.
✗  Weak initial post #1 — too vague and too short
Runlevels are old and systemd targets are the new version. They basically do the same thing but systemd is better because it starts things faster. Rescue mode is for when something breaks. I did the lab and changed the target and then changed it back. It worked fine.
~52 words ✗
Why this falls short
It is far below 150 words. No specific runlevel numbers or target names appear. "Something breaks" is not a scenario — a scenario names the specific problem. No commands are mentioned. There is nothing for a classmate to respond to or build on.
✗  Weak initial post #2 — only half the prompt answered
The mapping between runlevels and systemd targets is a one-to-one correspondence. Runlevel 0 is poweroff.target, runlevel 1 is rescue.target, runlevel 3 is multi-user.target, runlevel 5 is graphical.target, and runlevel 6 is reboot.target. Ubuntu Server defaults to multi-user.target, which I confirmed with systemctl get-default. The old /etc/init.d/ scripts still exist on Ubuntu as compatibility shims, which I thought was a thoughtful design choice since it prevents older scripts from completely breaking on modern systems. I read the DigitalOcean article on systemd units and it explained how the [Install] section of a unit file uses WantedBy= to link a service to a target, which is what creates the symlinks in /etc/systemd/system/multi-user.target.wants/.
~129 words ✗
Why this falls short
Part 1 is handled well — there are specific names, commands, and a reference to the reading. But Part 2 is missing entirely. The prompt asks for a real rescue-mode scenario, and this post never addresses it. Even if the word count were over 150, leaving out half the prompt costs significant points.

Peer Reply Examples

✓  Strong peer reply #1 — adds a new idea and asks a follow-up
Your /etc/fstab scenario is exactly the kind of situation I had in mind too. One thing I would add: once you fix the problem and reboot normally, it is worth running systemd-analyze blame to see if any services were affected by the bad mount entry. I found that command useful during the lab just to see which services take the longest to start — it makes the boot sequence feel a lot less like a black box. Did you notice whether rescue mode mounted your filesystem read-only or read-write? I had to remount it before I could write any changes, which added an extra step I was not expecting.
Why this earns full reply credit
It introduces a new tool (systemd-analyze blame) that is relevant to the discussion but not in the original post. It shares a specific experience (the read-only filesystem issue). It ends with a genuine question that the original poster can answer, keeping the conversation going.
✓  Strong peer reply #2 — offers an alternative scenario
I like the /etc/fstab example — that is a classic. Another scenario I have read about is a forgotten root password or a corrupted sudoers file. If you can no longer run sudo and you do not have another admin account, rescue mode gives you a root shell to either reset the password with passwd or restore /etc/sudoers with visudo. The DigitalOcean article mentioned that this is one of the main reasons rescue mode exists on servers. I had not connected that reading to the lab until I saw your post. Do you think there are situations where even rescue mode would not be enough to recover the system?
Why this earns full reply credit
It adds a distinct, technically accurate rescue scenario. It connects the classmate's post to the reading (DigitalOcean reference). It ends with a thought-provoking question. The tone is collaborative rather than just agreeable.
✗  Weak peer reply #1 — no substance
Great post! I had a similar experience with the lab and also found rescue mode kind of interesting.
Why this earns no credit
It agrees but adds nothing. There is no new idea, no question, no connection to the reading, and no specific detail. Reading this reply tells the original poster nothing they did not already know.
✗  Weak peer reply #2 — restates without adding
Yeah, runlevels and targets basically map to each other and it is good to know rescue mode for emergencies. Your post explained it well.
Why this earns no credit
It paraphrases the original post without adding anything. Complimenting a classmate is fine, but a reply that consists entirely of agreement and restatement is not substantive.
