CSC 106 — Week 1 — RSI Discussion

Discussion 1: Choosing and Installing Linux

Points: 15 CSLO 1 📅 Original post due Wednesday 📅 Replies due Sunday

Discussion Prompt

This week you installed Ubuntu Server from scratch. This discussion has two threads — respond to both in a single post.

Thread 1 — Your installation experience

You just installed Ubuntu Server from scratch. What surprised you about the process, or what was different from what you expected? Was there a step that felt confusing or that you had to repeat? Share at least one specific detail from your own installation experience.

Thread 2 — Distribution choice

In a professional setting, what factors would influence your choice of Linux distribution for a server? Think about things like long-term support, package availability, and community resources.

▶ What do "long-term support," "package availability," and "community resources" mean?

These terms come up constantly in professional Linux conversations. Here is what they mean in plain language:

  • Long-term support (LTS): Some Linux releases are designated LTS, meaning the publisher commits to providing security updates and bug fixes for a longer period — typically 5 years for Ubuntu LTS versus 9 months for a standard release. On a production server you cannot afford to reinstall the OS every year, so LTS releases are strongly preferred.
  • Package availability: How many software packages are available in the distribution's official repositories, and how current they are. A distribution with a large, well-maintained package ecosystem means you can install almost any server software with apt or dnf without resorting to third-party sources.
  • Community resources: The size and quality of documentation, forums, Stack Overflow answers, and tutorials written for that distribution. If something breaks at 2 AM, you want to be able to find a relevant answer quickly. Ubuntu and RHEL-based distributions have the largest communities for server use.

Other factors worth mentioning: cost (RHEL requires a subscription; Ubuntu LTS and Rocky Linux are free), support contracts (important for enterprise environments), and familiarity within a team.

What makes a reply "substantive"?

A substantive reply engages with something specific your classmate wrote. It does at least one of the following:

  • Adds information or a perspective your classmate did not mention
  • Asks a follow-up question that invites them to go deeper
  • Shares a contrasting experience and explains why it might be different
  • Offers a constructive alternative to a choice or opinion they expressed
✓ Substantive

"You mentioned the partitioning screen confused you — I had the same reaction. What helped me was running lsblk after the install to see how the disk actually ended up laid out. Did you notice whether the installer created a separate swap partition or a swapfile? I'm curious whether that varies by the options you pick."

✗ Not substantive

"Great post! I agree that Ubuntu is a good choice for servers. I also had some issues during the installation but managed to get through it. Good point about the support."

What Good Looks Like

The examples below are representative student responses — not model answers. They illustrate the difference between responses that earn full credit and responses that do not.

Original Post Examples

✓ Strong original post
"Installing Ubuntu Server was more disorienting than I expected, mostly because the installer is text-based — no mouse, just arrow keys and Enter. The step that actually tripped me up was partitioning. I chose guided partitioning and then panicked when I saw a message saying it would use the entire virtual disk. Once I read it more carefully and confirmed the layout (a small EFI partition, a swap partition, and a large root partition), it made sense and I moved on. For a production server I would stick with Ubuntu Server LTS or move to Rocky Linux depending on the organization. Ubuntu 24.04 LTS gets five years of standard support plus additional security updates, which matters when you cannot afford to rebuild a server every couple of years. I also found that almost every troubleshooting article I turned to during the install was written with Ubuntu in mind, which makes a real difference when something breaks. That said, in an enterprise environment where a vendor support contract matters, I would seriously look at RHEL or Rocky Linux as a free RHEL-compatible alternative."
Why this earns full credit:
  • Addresses both threads (personal experience and distribution choice)
  • Names a specific step (partitioning) and explains what happened
  • Uses accurate technical vocabulary (EFI partition, swap, LTS, RHEL)
  • Discusses trade-offs rather than just stating a preference
  • Well over 150 words
✗ Weak original post
"The installation went pretty well for me. I had a few issues but figured them out. Ubuntu is a popular choice for servers because it's well-supported and easy to use. I would choose Ubuntu for most server tasks because it's what we're learning in this class. Overall I think Linux is better for servers than Windows because it's free and open source."
Why this earns partial credit at best:
  • Under 150 words
  • No specific detail from the installation experience — "a few issues" is vague
  • Does not engage with the distribution choice factors in the prompt (LTS, packages, community)
  • Circular reasoning: "I'd choose Ubuntu because that's what we're learning"
  • The Linux vs. Windows point is off-topic

Peer Reply Examples

The following replies are responding to the strong original post shown above.

✓ Strong reply
"The partitioning step caught me off guard too. What helped me was running lsblk right after the install finished so I could actually see the partitions that were created and confirm the layout matched what I approved. Did you happen to notice whether the installer created a swap partition or a swapfile? My install created a swapfile instead, and I'm not sure if that's a default that changed in 24.04 or something specific to my setup. Your point about community resources resonates with me. I was using Rocky Linux briefly last semester and the documentation quality is noticeably thinner than Ubuntu's — especially for newer packages. I think for a class environment Ubuntu is an easy call, but I'm curious whether you'd feel differently if the scenario called for FIPS compliance or an environment that requires a specific vendor's support."
Why this earns full credit:
  • Responds to something specific the classmate wrote (partitioning, community resources)
  • Shares a related experience and an observation (lsblk, swapfile vs. swap partition)
  • Asks a genuine follow-up question that invites a deeper response
  • Introduces a new angle (FIPS compliance) without going off-topic
✗ Weak reply
"Great post! I agree that Ubuntu is a good choice for servers. I also had some trouble during the installation but got through it eventually. Good point about the support contracts."
Why this does not earn credit:
  • Does not engage with anything specific from the classmate's post
  • "I also had some trouble" — no detail, no shared experience
  • "Good point about the support contracts" — just repeating, not extending
  • Adds no new information, perspective, or question
